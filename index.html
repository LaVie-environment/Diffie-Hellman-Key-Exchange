Diffie-Hellman Interactive Demo

How can two people share a secret in public without anyone finding it out?

Every person deserves the right to a private conversation, even in a world where someone is always watching. Imagine you and a friend are mixing paint colors in public. You each start with the same base color, add your own secret color privately, then swap your mixtures openly. Even though a hacker (someone who can see every paint can you send) sees every can you exchange, they can never recreate your secret because they never see your private color.

That is exactly what the Diffie-Hellman key exchange algorithm does. It uses clock arithmetic — mixing numbers together is easy, but reversing it is nearly impossible — so two people can arrive at the same shared secret completely in the open, without ever revealing it.

In this interactive demo, Alice and Bob are two fictitious people who want to create a shared secret key so they can encrypt their messages. Eve is the eavesdropper — she can see everything Alice and Bob send to each other, but she will never be able to work out the secret they created together.
